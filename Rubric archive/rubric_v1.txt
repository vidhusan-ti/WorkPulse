# High-Bar Portfolio Entry Rubric (Trilogy Innovations)

`rubric_version: 2.0` - conversation-level unit, Trilogy-calibrated, eligibility-gates-plus-depth-score model. Set `rubric_version` in every graded output to match.

Use this rubric to identify only the rare transcript-backed conversations that deserve to become portfolio entries.

The unit of judgment and the unit that gets labeled is the whole conversation, never a single prompt or a single turn. A conversation is the full thread: every user turn, every assistant response, the artifacts it changed, and the latest outcome. You assign exactly one `label` and one `score` to the conversation as a whole.

Default label is `below_bar`. Use `above_bar` only when the conversation as a whole proves a non-obvious human judgment move that a third-party reviewer could use to assess the person's intellectual capability.

## Unit of Judgment: The Conversation

Grade conversations, not prompts. Do not pick out a single prompt and label it; read the whole conversation and decide whether the conversation earns `above_bar`.

- A conversation is `above_bar` when, read end to end, it demonstrates qualifying human judgment, normally anchored by one or more clearly user-owned high-bar moves that are visible in the artifact or outcome.
- A single strong move can carry a conversation, but only if that move plus its surrounding context can fill the full case-study anatomy: situation, stakes, human move, evidence, alternatives rejected, artifact change, and outcome. A lone clever one-liner with thin context is `transcript_highlight`, not `above_bar`.
- If a conversation covers several unrelated tasks, judge it by its strongest self-contained work episode and record the turns that anchor that episode. Still assign exactly one label and one score to the conversation.
- A conversation with many competent but ordinary turns and no rare move is `below_bar`, no matter how long or how polished the individual prompts are.
- When citing evidence for the label, quote the specific turns that drove it, but the verdict still belongs to the conversation, not to those turns in isolation.

## Who This Is For: Trilogy Innovations

This rubric grades the work of people inside Trilogy Innovations, where the baseline talent is already high. Recruits pass through Trilogy University, a problem-solving gauntlet modeled on elite training programs, and the culture rewards a specific kind of thinker: someone who applies disruptive, first-principles thinking to enterprise-software problems instead of accepting the conventional path. Trilogy people operate as the R&D arm for their clients, manage massive scale at low cost without sacrificing quality, and treat AI as the core leverage that makes that possible.

Because the population is already strong, the bar for `above_bar` must be raised, not lowered. A move that would impress from an average engineer can still be `below_bar` here if it only shows competent execution. The portfolio exists to surface the rare moves that separate an exceptional Trilogy problem-solver from a merely capable one.

Trilogy-defining traits the portfolio should capture:

- Disruptive, first-principles reframing: rejecting "how it is normally done" and redesigning from the real constraint, scale, or cost target. Conventional, textbook-correct answers are not enough.
- Founder-grade ownership: the person drives the decision the way an owner would, not the way a ticket-taker would. At Trilogy University recruits own a product end to end while the founder plays VC; the portfolio should show that same ownership of the problem and its outcome.
- Leverage at scale: using AI, automation, deterministic workflows, or reuse to get large output, cost savings, and quality at the same time. This is the opposite of conventional development, where scale usually costs quality and money.
- Business value: connecting the engineering move to a real outcome (revenue, cost, client problem, risk, time-to-value), not just technical elegance.
- AI as a force multiplier, not a crutch: directing, correcting, and bounding the model while keeping human judgment in control of the decision.
- Evidence over assertion: demanding proof, concrete constraints, and measurable bars before committing, consistent with a meritocracy that measures everything.

## Purpose

This rubric must not collect "good prompts." It must collect portfolio-worthy evidence of thinking.

A portfolio entry should show that the person saw something important, challenged or reframed the obvious path, used the LLM as one tool among others, and moved the work toward a better decision, artifact, architecture, or implementation. The entry should feel like a case study of judgment, not a prompt-writing example.

The highest standard is two questions, and both must clearly pass:

> 1. Could an evaluator see a non-obvious human insight here that an LLM would not have produced from the project docs alone?
> 2. Does the move show what makes a Trilogy person, that is disruptive or first-principles thinking, founder-grade ownership, leverage at scale, AI used as a multiplier, or a clear line to business value, beyond competent execution?

If either answer is not clearly yes, mark `below_bar`.

## What To Review

Read the whole conversation before grading. Do not grade from a single prompt.

- The full thread of user turns and assistant responses, start to finish.
- The strongest user-owned move or moves, plus the turns immediately before and after them, so you can see who introduced the insight.
- How the thinking evolved across the conversation: what the user challenged, corrected, or decided as the thread developed.
- Any project artifact affected by the conversation: IPD, ITD, design doc, code, research note, architecture decision, test, implementation plan, or review.
- The latest response or outcome, because the value may only be visible after the conversation develops.

Never grade a single prompt or excerpt in isolation unless the source conversation is unavailable. If the full conversation is unavailable, set `mentor_review_needed = true` and avoid `above_bar` unless the available excerpt contains overwhelming evidence.

## Labels

- `above_bar` - Portfolio-worthy. The conversation can become a real portfolio entry with minimal editing because it proves original human judgment, context, artifact impact, and learning.
- `transcript_highlight` - Useful conversation, with a strong moment or good collaboration pattern, but not enough as a whole for a portfolio entry.
- `below_bar` - Routine, generic, assistant-led, or insufficiently evidenced conversation.

Only `above_bar` should be used for final portfolio entries. `transcript_highlight` may be used for coaching or raw evidence, but it should not be presented as portfolio work.

## Hard Gates (Eligibility)

Hard gates are pass/fail. They decide whether a conversation is even eligible for `above_bar`; they are not scored. If any hard gate fails, the best possible label is `transcript_highlight`. Gates may overlap with scoring dimensions by design: a gate is the floor (the thing must be present at all), while the matching dimension measures how strongly it is demonstrated.

- Full-conversation judgment: the reviewer read the whole conversation and can explain how the user's thinking evolved across it.
- Human-original move: somewhere in the conversation the user introduced the key insight, concern, reframing, standard, or decision; it was not supplied first by the assistant.
- Assistant-delta proof: across the conversation the user changed the direction, quality, or standard of the assistant's work rather than only accepting, formatting, or requesting expansion.
- Real project stakes: the conversation is grounded in a specific product, workflow, user, client, system, artifact, or business decision.
- Non-obvious insight: the strongest move is not something a generic LLM would likely produce from the docs with a prompt like "write a good insight."

Artifact impact, evidence grounding, and reflection are not hard gates; they are scored dimensions below. A strong conversation will still usually show them, and they remain part of the case-study anatomy required before an entry can be published.

## Scoring Dimensions

Score the conversation as a whole. Award 1 point only when the dimension is clearly and substantively demonstrated somewhere in the conversation. Do not award points for keyword presence, politeness, length, or a well-structured prompt. Sum the ten dimensions to get a depth score from 0 to 10.

1. `context_chain` - The conversation makes the situation before the move clear and why the moment mattered.
2. `ownership` - The user personally owns the thinking move like an owner, not a ticket-taker; the assistant did not hand it to them first.
3. `originality` - The insight is non-obvious, specific, and unlikely to be produced by a generic LLM from the source docs alone.
4. `reframe` - The user changes the problem definition, decision frame, success standard, or architecture direction, ideally from first principles rather than the conventional default.
5. `evidence` - The user provides, requests, challenges, or applies concrete facts, constraints, or measurable bars needed to judge.
6. `alternatives` - The conversation compares meaningful paths, rejected options, or competing models, including build vs buy vs integrate.
7. `tradeoffs` - The user makes cost, risk, friction, reversibility, timing, scale, or stakeholder tension explicit.
8. `artifact_impact` - The conversation materially improves a doc, design, system, plan, implementation, or review, and where relevant moves the work toward a real business outcome.
9. `leverage` - The user uses the LLM, automation, deterministic workflows, or reuse to multiply output, cost-efficiency, and quality at once, without outsourcing judgment.
10. `reflection` - The conversation captures learning, changed assumptions, remaining uncertainty, or the next validation step.

The depth score does not by itself decide the label. See `From Score to Label` for how the hard gates and the depth score combine. A 10/10 conversation should feel exceptional, even relative to a population of strong Trilogy problem-solvers.

## Scoring Anchors

Use these anchors to avoid generous scoring. Remember the population is already strong, so "good for a normal engineer" is the `0` side of the line here.

- `0` - Missing, generic, assistant-supplied, purely implied, present only as rubric language, conventional or textbook-correct without a distinctive angle, or not visible without charity.
- `1` - Specific, user-owned, grounded in transcript context, and strong enough to change how a skilled Trilogy collaborator would understand or continue the work.

Do not award half credit. If the judge cannot quote or paraphrase the user's original move, artifact impact, and why it is non-obvious, score the relevant dimension `0`.

## From Score to Label

Apply these steps in order. Hard gates are eligibility; the depth score is quality.

1. Check the hard gates. If any hard gate fails, the conversation cannot be `above_bar`.
2. Compute the depth score by summing the ten scoring dimensions (0 to 10).
3. Confirm the Trilogy bar: the conversation shows disruptive or first-principles thinking, founder-grade ownership, leverage at scale, AI used as a multiplier, or a clear line to business value, beyond competent execution.
4. Assign exactly one label:
   - `above_bar` - all hard gates pass, the Trilogy bar is cleared, and depth score >= 8.
   - `transcript_highlight` - not `above_bar`, but the conversation has at least one genuine user-owned move of real value, and either the depth score is 4 to 7 or a single hard gate is the only thing blocking it.
   - `below_bar` - depth score <= 3, or the conversation has no genuine user-owned move, or more than one hard gate fails.

A depth score of 8 or 9 should be a clear, defensible portfolio entry. Reserve a perfect 10 for conversations that feel exceptional even among strong Trilogy problem-solvers. When a conversation lands exactly on a boundary, choose the lower label.

## Assistant-Delta Check

This is mandatory for every conversation. Apply it to the strongest user-owned move or moves in the thread.

Ask:

1. What did the assistant already say before the user's strongest move?
2. What did the user add that was not already present?
3. Across the conversation, did the user merely ask for rewriting, formatting, more detail, or execution?
4. Did the user challenge, correct, narrow, validate, or reframe the work?
5. Would the final insight still exist if the user had not made this move?

If the assistant supplied the insight and the user only asked to rewrite it, the conversation is `below_bar` or at most `transcript_highlight`.

## LLM-Reproducibility Test

Before approving, run this mental test:

> If an LLM were given the same project docs and asked to "write a strong portfolio insight," would it likely invent this same entry?

If yes, fail it. A portfolio entry must depend on human observation from the messy working context: a discovered contradiction, a real constraint, a user-specific workflow, a rejected assumption, a subtle edge case, or a decision that emerged through iteration.

## Portfolio Entry Anatomy

An `above_bar` conversation should be convertible into a case study with these parts:

- `situation` - What was happening before the insight?
- `stakes` - Why did this matter?
- `human_move` - What did the user uniquely notice, challenge, decide, or reframe?
- `evidence_used` - What facts, constraints, examples, APIs, docs, or failures grounded the move?
- `alternatives_rejected` - What plausible path did the user avoid or replace?
- `artifact_changed` - What IPD, ITD, design, architecture, plan, implementation, or standard changed?
- `outcome_or_learning` - What became clearer, safer, faster, more correct, or more strategic?
- `why_not_generic_llm` - Why this required context-specific human judgment.

If these fields cannot be filled honestly from the transcript, do not call it a portfolio entry.

## Insight Types

Classify the conversation's strongest human move as one of:

- `root_problem_reframe` - changes the apparent task into the real underlying problem.
- `first_principles_disruption` - rejects the conventional or industry-standard approach and rebuilds the solution from the underlying constraint, scale, or cost target.
- `assistant_correction` - catches a wrong, shallow, or overconfident assistant direction.
- `domain_specific_edge_case` - identifies a case that breaks the generic solution because of project-specific workflow or domain rules.
- `build_vs_buy_judgment` - narrows what should be bought, built, integrated, or rejected based on differentiated value.
- `architecture_boundary_design` - defines system boundaries, ownership, data flow, failure modes, or integration shape.
- `leverage_design` - designs AI, automation, deterministic workflows, or reuse that multiplies output, cost-efficiency, and quality at scale rather than adding manual or unreliable effort.
- `business_value_link` - ties a technical move directly to a client outcome, revenue, cost, risk, or time-to-value decision.
- `evidence_standard_raise` - demands proof, source validation, POC criteria, or measurable decision evidence.
- `scope_and_tradeoff_control` - prevents overbuilding or underbuilding by naming the right depth, phase, or constraint.
- `artifact_transformation` - turns messy discussion into a durable decision artifact that changes execution.
- `principled_pushback` - rejects a plausible path because it violates the real goal, user need, or review standard.

If none of these are present anywhere in the conversation, the conversation is usually `below_bar`.

## Automatic Fail Patterns

A conversation is not `above_bar` if its overall arc matches any of these, unless it also contains a rare human-original move that carries the whole conversation.

- The conversation is mainly the user asking the assistant to write an IPD, ITD, plan, research note, or market analysis.
- The conversation is dominated by rewrite-only requests, grammar cleanup, tone changes, formatting, summarization, or "make this better."
- The user's turns are prompt-engineering artifacts that mechanically include goal, context, constraints, and output format, with no real judgment.
- The conversation is information retrieval where the insight is in the assistant's research, not the user's judgment.
- The user asks for options without ever naming their own hypothesis, constraint, rejection, or decision impact.
- The user accepts the assistant's recommendation without validation, pushback, or a changed standard.
- Turns that sound strong only because they contain words like "tradeoff," "risk," "architecture," or "evidence."
- Generic architecture or product questions that could apply to many projects.
- A conversation whose portfolio story can only be created by adding invented context not present in the transcript.
- A conversation where the assistant's contribution is consistently more impressive than the user's.
- A conversation whose strongest user move is useful but ordinary project management, not distinctive intellectual judgment.
- The conversation accepts the conventional or industry-standard approach without testing whether Trilogy's scale, cost, or AI-leverage model demands a different design.
- The conversation adds manual steps, headcount, or an unreliable LLM-in-the-loop where a deterministic or automated workflow was the higher-leverage move.
- A technically sound conversation with no line to business value, client outcome, or cost and scale impact.
- Competent execution that any strong Trilogy engineer would produce by default, with no rare or disruptive judgment anywhere in the thread.
- A conversation that contains one good prompt but is otherwise routine; this is `transcript_highlight`, not `above_bar`.

## Above-Bar Standard

`above_bar` requires:

- All hard gates pass.
- Depth score is at least 8/10.
- The conversation is judged as a whole from the full transcript, not from a single prompt excerpt.
- The user's contribution is visibly original relative to the assistant responses in the conversation.
- The insight is grounded in the actual project and would not be obvious to a generic LLM.
- The conversation changes an artifact, decision, architecture, or execution path.
- The conversation clears the Trilogy bar: it shows disruptive or first-principles thinking, founder-grade ownership, leverage at scale, AI used as a multiplier, or a clear line to business value, not just competent execution.
- The rest of the conversation does not undercut the strongest move; the user keeps ownership of the thinking through the thread.
- The conversation can be written as a third-person portfolio case study without exaggerating the user's contribution.

When in doubt, mark `transcript_highlight` or `below_bar`. False positives are worse than missing a borderline good conversation, especially in a population where most work is already competent.

## Required Diagnosis Fields

Each grade is for one conversation and must include:

- `conversation_ref` - identifier or path of the conversation being graded.
- `label` - `above_bar`, `transcript_highlight`, or `below_bar`, for the conversation as a whole.
- `depth_score` - integer 0 to 10, the sum of the ten scoring dimensions.
- `hard_gates_failed` - list of failed eligibility gates, or `none`.
- `trilogy_bar_cleared` - true or false, with one line on which trait it shows.
- `insight_type` - the conversation's strongest insight type above, or `none`.
- `key_turns` - the specific turns in the conversation that drove the label.
- `context_chain` - what happened in the conversation before its strongest move.
- `assistant_delta` - what the user added beyond prior assistant output.
- `human_original_move` - the user's strongest original contribution in the conversation.
- `artifact_impact` - what changed in the work product or decision path.
- `why_not_generic_llm` - why this was not a generic model-generated insight.
- `missing_move` - the highest-leverage addition that would make the conversation portfolio-worthy.
- `mentor_review_needed` - true when context, originality, or domain meaning is uncertain.

## Coaching Requirements

Coaching must not only produce a "better prompt." For weak conversations, coach toward a better thinking conversation and a better artifact.

For `below_bar` or `transcript_highlight` conversations, include:

- The missing hard gates.
- Whether the weakness is missing context, missing ownership, assistant-supplied insight, missing artifact impact, missing evidence, missing outcome, or missing reflection.
- A short explanation tied to the actual conversation.
- A stronger next move the user could make, such as a validation question, edge-case test, POC criterion, decision log, artifact revision, or evidence request.

Do not turn a weak conversation into a polished fake portfolio entry. If the conversation does not prove the insight, say so.

## Calibration Dataset

Maintain a mentor-labeled dataset where each row is one conversation:

- `conversation_ref`
- `key_turns`
- `prior_context_summary`
- `assistant_before`
- `assistant_after`
- `artifact_before`
- `artifact_after`
- `mentor_label`
- `mentor_score`
- `mentor_reason`
- `assistant_delta`
- `hard_gates_failed`
- `expected_insight_type`
- `rubric_version`

The rubric is not production-grade until at least 50 real conversations have mentor labels, including obvious failures, transcript highlights, borderline cases, and true `above_bar` portfolio entries.

Success target:

- zero known false positives in approved portfolio entries
- mentor agreement on `above_bar` decisions above 90%
- all disagreements categorized by missing gate or ambiguous originality

## Calibration Loop

Treat the rubric as a measuring instrument.

1. Collect 50-100 real conversations, not isolated prompts.
2. For each conversation, keep the full thread and its latest assistant response.
3. Label each conversation manually as `above_bar`, `transcript_highlight`, or `below_bar`.
4. Audit false positives first.
5. Re-check assistant-delta and LLM-reproducibility on every approved conversation.
6. Update hard gates and examples based on misses.
7. Version the rubric whenever the judge prompt, model, or examples change.

## Source Principles

- Portfolio entries are case studies of judgment, not prompt collections.
- Strong AI use is necessary but not sufficient for portfolio value.
- A good portfolio entry proves ownership, context, decision quality, evidence, impact, and reflection.
- The key question is not "was the prompt good?" but "did the person make a rare, context-specific intellectual move that reflects how a Trilogy problem-solver thinks?"
- The bar is calibrated to a strong population. Competent execution is the baseline, not the achievement; the portfolio captures disruptive thinking, ownership, leverage at scale, and business impact on top of that baseline.
- The system should reject weak entries aggressively. A small number of undeniable entries is better than a long list of polished but ordinary prompts.
