## Cross-Candidate Patterns

1. **Well-structured task descriptions keep almost qualifying.** Abdul and Bharath both write detailed, well-organised task specs that look above bar on surface. They consistently fail the "semantic novelty" criterion: the content could have been generated by the LLM given the project context. Formatting and structure are not insight.

2. **"Implement the plan as specified" is the most common near-miss trigger.** Once a plan document exists, execution-direction prompts carry no independent information. This appeared in Abdul (legal intelligence), Bharath (all module builds), and occasionally Arleif.

3. **Arleif stands out for architectural critique.** Unlike the others, Arleif frequently contributes at the system design level — catching incorrect assumptions, making trade-off calls, proposing simplifications. His above-bar count is higher because more of his prompts are genuine design contributions rather than implementation instructions.

4. **Vidhusan's transcripts are heavily LLM-driven.** Most sessions are short, reactive, or involve the LLM driving the educational or research content. The one above-bar prompt (roleplay inverted coach) is a genuine product insight; everything else either asks for execution, interpretation of others' feedback, or minor formatting/direction corrections.

5. **Bharath is technically competent but prompt-insightful only occasionally.** His projects are serious (multi-tenant agent, full Backtrace pipeline) but his prompts rarely contribute independent architectural insight — the specs are provided to him or emerge from prior LLM responses. The above-bar windows are those where he identifies a gap or assumption rather than describing a requirement.

## Rubric Criteria Hard to Apply Consistently

- **"The key insight should be from the user and not from the LLM"** — hardest to apply when the conversation is collaborative and iterative. Arleif's work in particular involves tight back-and-forth; distinguishing "user synthesises from LLM output" (near-miss) from "user independently identifies, LLM confirms" (above bar) required reading the conversation sequence carefully, not just the prompt text.

- **"Efficient, not reaching the output by repeating the same prompts"** — Bharath's situation room and coach sessions often involve near-correct answers followed by slight nudges. The rubric says repeating prompts to reach an insight disqualifies it, but judging whether a nudge was "necessary" or "a repeat" is contextual.

- **Distinguishing restatement from synthesis** — Vidhusan frequently restates LLM-provided scenario content in his own words. Even when the restatement shows comprehension, it is not novel contribution. This was consistently applied as disqualifying.
